Blood Donation Knowledge Hub

History of Blood Donation

Modern blood transfusions began in the early 1800s; the first documented successful human-to-human transfusion happened in 1818 by Dr. James Blundell for a postpartum patient. In the 20th century, Karl Landsteiner discovered blood groups in 1901, enabling safe matching.

In India, the National Blood Policy (2002) and National Blood Transfusion Council (NBTC) established voluntary non-remunerated donation systems with strong quality control.

First recorded blood donor: In 1818, the first successful blood donor was a patient to patient transfusion by Dr. Blundell; another early pioneering donor was a British soldier in the Boer War.

Blood Types & Compatibility

  • Type O−: Universal donor (can give to all, rare)
  • Type O+: Most common; can donate to all positives
  • Type A−/A+: Can give to A and AB recipients
  • Type B−/B+: Can give to B and AB recipients
  • Type AB−/AB+: Universal plasma donors; AB+ receives all types

Rh factor (+/−) is critical for transfusions; mismatch can cause reactions.

How it Started in India

Blood donation in India formally began in the mid-20th century with voluntary donor clubs and hospital-based blood banks. Over time, government campaigns and NSS camps increased awareness and participation. Today, camps are common on college campuses and during festivals.

Indian Laws & Regulations

  • Voluntary, non-remunerated blood donation is the cornerstone of Indian policy.
  • National Blood Policy (2002) requires all blood banks to follow strict donor screening and testing.
  • WHO guidelines require transfusion safety, traceability, and infection screening (HIV, Hepatitis B/C, malaria).
  • Donor age: 18–65; weight >=50 kg; hemoglobin >=12.5 g/dL; no high-risk behavior.
  • Regulations enforced by National AIDS Control Organization (NACO) and NBTC.

Indian Laws & Regulations

  • National Blood Policy (India) ensures voluntary blood donation and safe transfusion.
  • WHO guidelines require screening, storage, and donor counselling.
  • Blood banks must follow standardized testing and a robust traceability process.

Who Can Donate Blood (India Guidelines)

  • Age: 18–65 years
  • Weight: ≥50 kg
  • Hemoglobin: ≥12.5 g/dL

Who Cannot Donate

  • Chronic diseases (uncontrolled diabetes, heart issues, cancer)
  • Recent infections, surgery, or hospitalization
  • Pregnancy or recent childbirth
  • Certain medications and infections like HIV/hepatitis

Tips to Increase Blood Levels

  • Iron-rich foods: spinach, beetroot, dates, pomegranate
  • Vitamin C to improve iron absorption
  • Stay hydrated before and after donation
  • Follow doctor guidance for nutrition and rest
